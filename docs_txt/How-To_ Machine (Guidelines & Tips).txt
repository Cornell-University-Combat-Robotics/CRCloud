Common issues

My tap broke!

Oopsie! That sucks. Unfortunately, this probably means that your part is no longer usable (though this may vary on a case-by-case basis). Since the tap is made of a much harder metal than your drill bits, there‚Äôs no way to drill through it. You may now take this part home and put it on your wall of failures. 

There are 4 common culprits that lead to tap breaking:

You used too much pressure: When you tap a hole, you should not be pushing downward at all. If you have to use force to get your tap to cut threads, then something is wrong. The only force you should apply is horizontal to twist the tap in a circle. 

You used too much torque: Try not to use a tap handle that is way too big for the hole you‚Äôre tapping. There‚Äôs no real metric for this, just try to find a tap handle that feels right. If you use one that‚Äôs way too big, there‚Äôs a bigger chance that small errors can cause your tap to break (like not being completely aligned or hitting the bottom of the hole). 

You continued tapping even when you reached the bottom of the hole: This aligns with #1. Do not use force to go down! When you reach the bottom of the hole or feel any resistance, stop. If you use a ‚Äúbottoming tap‚Äù (which looks like the first picture) as opposed to a ‚Äútaper tap‚Äù (which looks like the second picture) then when you hit the bottom of the hole, your tap will stop instantly.

			



Your tap wasn‚Äôt straight: This usually happens if you‚Äôre tapping by hand (which is a bit risky, and I don‚Äôt recommend it). If you absolutely must tap by hand, always have someone else watching you from multiple angles to make sure you‚Äôre tapping straight.

You didn‚Äôt whack it with the hammer hard enough! Give it a really good smack. Don‚Äôt be shy, you know you want to. Make sure to smack it when it‚Äôs secure in the vise‚Äîif you hit it and it‚Äôs not secure, it‚Äôll just go flying (or make a really loud noise).
